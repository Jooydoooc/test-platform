# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Two roles, one private classroom:

- **Students** — the teacher's own English learners, across levels (beginner to advanced). They open Lexora to practise, take assigned tests, review their mistakes, and see how they rank against classmates. Often on a phone, in short sessions, sometimes between classes. They are motivated but need momentum: clear next steps, fast feedback, visible progress.
- **The teacher (owner)** — manages students and groups, uploads tasks and tests, adds textbook-based practice, checks results, and tracks class progress. Wants control and a clear read on who's doing the work.

Lexora is not public. There is no signup funnel, no marketing surface. Everyone who lands here already belongs.

## Product Purpose

Lexora is a private practice-and-progress platform for one teacher's students, focused on **General English** (not IELTS): grammar, reading, listening, and vocabulary. It exists to make deliberate practice feel structured and motivating — teacher-selected materials, textbook exercises, graded tests, mistake review, progress tracking, and friendly competition (ranks, badges, leaderboards). Success looks like students coming back on their own between lessons because the platform makes progress visible and the next step obvious.

## Brand Personality

Professional, modern, clean, motivating, organised, student-friendly — serious but not boring. The voice is a good teacher's: encouraging and direct, never gimmicky. It respects the student's time and intelligence. Confidence without corporate stiffness; warmth without childishness.

Three words: **focused, encouraging, credible.**

## Anti-references

- **Childish quiz sites** (Kahoot-style confetti, cartoon mascots, comic fonts, rainbow buttons). Motivation here is earned progress, not noise.
- **Generic public course marketplaces** (Udemy/Coursera-style dense catalogs, stock-photo heroes, "enroll now" pressure).
- **Generic AI-generated dashboards** — the SaaS-cream body, tiny tracked-uppercase eyebrow on every section, identical icon-heading-text card grids, hero-metric templates. If it looks auto-generated, it's wrong.

## Design Principles

1. **Progress is the product.** Every screen should answer "how am I doing / what's next?" Scores, streaks, and completion are first-class, not afterthoughts.
2. **One confident accent, used with intent.** Indigo is identity and action; teal marks progress and success; amber celebrates achievement. Color means something — it is never decoration.
3. **Motivating, not noisy.** Reward effort with restraint: a badge, a filled bar, a rank change. No confetti, no shouting.
4. **Fast and frictionless on a phone.** Students practise in short bursts. Touch targets, instant feedback, and quick paths to the next task come first.
5. **Teacher-grade clarity.** Data (results, groups, rankings) reads cleanly and truthfully. No vanity metrics.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG 2.1 AA. Body text ≥4.5:1 contrast; large text ≥3:1. Visible keyboard focus on every interactive element (brand focus ring). Minimum 44px touch targets on mobile. Respect `prefers-reduced-motion`. Don't rely on color alone to convey pass/fail — pair with text or icon. Font size never locked; pinch-zoom allowed.
