# No AI Style Rules

Lexora must not look or sound AI-generated — this governs **all platform text**, student-facing and teacher-facing alike (dashboard alerts, notifications, result pages, empty states, everything).

## On AI itself — an important distinction

Lexora genuinely uses AI internally (Writing and Speaking grading, per `PRACTICE_TYPES.md`). This document is not a rule against using AI — it's a rule against *sounding* like generic AI marketing copy. The platform never announces or markets its own AI use in its UI: no "AI-powered feedback," no "smart algorithm recommends," no "powered by advanced AI." Feedback is just called **Feedback**. A recommendation is just shown, not narrated as intelligent.

## Avoid

- Generic marketing phrases
- Fake enthusiasm
- Robotic text
- Overused words like *unlock, empower, transform, revolutionize*
- Long, perfect-sounding paragraphs
- Meaningless slogans
- Random gradients
- Glowing cards
- Template-looking sections
- Childish quiz design
- **Emoji in system-generated text** — no 🎉 🔥 💪 in notifications, dashboards, or results. If visual emphasis is needed, use a plain icon, not an emoji.

## Use

- Clear instructions
- Short, useful sentences
- Natural teacher-like language
- Specific learning-related wording
- Practical dashboard text
- Simple, professional design
- **Exact numbers, not vague qualifiers** — "improved by 12%," never "improved by over 10%!" or "significantly improved."

## Honesty in dynamic text — including negative and neutral results

Every example below in the original draft was a positive result. That's not enough on its own: praising a flat or declining score to sound encouraging would itself be the fake positivity this document exists to prevent. Dynamic text follows the same plain, factual tone regardless of whether the result is good, flat, or bad:

- Positive: *"Your grammar score improved by 12%."*
- Flat: *"Your grammar score is steady this month."*
- Negative: *"Your grammar score dropped by 5% this month. Review these topics."*

None of these are softened, exaggerated, or shamed — they state the fact and, where useful, point at a next action.

## Notifications and reminders — the highest-risk zone

Streak reminders and "needs attention" flags are where most apps default to either fake urgency or accidental shame. Neither is acceptable:

- Avoid: *"Don't lose your streak! 🔥"* (fake urgency)
- Avoid: *"You're falling behind!"* (shame, no action)
- Use: *"You haven't practised in 5 days. Review 5 weak words to continue your streak."* (factual, specific, actionable)

## Empty and error states

These get the same plain treatment as everything else — no forced enthusiasm to fill a blank screen:

- Avoid: *"All caught up! 🎉 You're a superstar learner!"*
- Use: *"No tasks assigned yet. Check back after your next lesson, or try Extra Practice."*

## Good examples

- Complete today's vocabulary task.
- Your grammar score improved by 12%.
- Review these five weak words.
- You are ranked 3rd for weekly activity.
- Your strongest skill this month is vocabulary.
- You missed 4 questions in Present Simple.

## Bad examples

- Unlock your full potential with our revolutionary learning system.
- Transform your educational journey with powerful AI-driven excellence.
- Empower your success through a world-class digital experience.

The platform should feel like it was designed by a serious English teacher and a professional product designer — not a startup landing page.
